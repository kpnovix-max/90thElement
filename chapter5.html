The Long Silence
1974 – 2011
Chapter Five
Pokhran,
May 1974
May 18, 1974
"They called it Smiling Buddha.
It was the smile that cost them everything."

The Thar Desert in May is a landscape that has reduced itself to essentials. The scrub that manages to survive in it through the winter months has by then largely given up the pretence of greenness and stands in the pale distances as a collection of grey-brown suggestions — not quite dead, not quite alive, existing at the exact threshold between the two states that the desert demands of everything that chooses to remain in it. The sand is not the golden sand of imagination. It is pinkish, dusty, fine as talc in the dry places and surprisingly hard-packed where the rock is close to the surface, and it holds the heat of the day into the evening with a thoroughness that makes the nights, which can be cold in other seasons, warm and still and full of the particular silence of a landscape where there is nothing to break the sound but distance. The sky above the Thar in May is enormous — not the intimate sky of forests and cities, not the framed sky of valleys and hills, but the absolute sky of a flat open place that has nothing to put between itself and the horizon in any direction, and the stars at night in that sky are the stars as they actually are, uncorrected by the light of human settlement, and they are, seen this way, overwhelming in their quantity and their indifference.

The Pokhran test range occupied a portion of this desert in the Jaisalmer district of Rajasthan, a site chosen for the obvious practical reasons that a government chooses remote desert locations for activities it does not wish to be observable from populated areas. It had been a military installation since the 1960s. In the early months of 1974 it had been, in addition to its ordinary military function, the site of considerable preparatory activity that had been conducted with the specific care of people who understood that the activity they were preparing for would be watched very closely from a large number of directions and that the watching needed to begin only after the preparation was complete.

The preparation had been completed. The watching had not yet begun.

At eight fifteen on the morning of May the eighteenth, 1974, a device was detonated at the bottom of a shaft sunk one hundred and seven metres into the desert floor. The detonation was nuclear. The yield was estimated at approximately eight kilotons — smaller than the Hiroshima bomb, larger than the minimum threshold required to demonstrate that the device had functioned as designed. The ground above the shaft rose slightly, a slow upheaval visible from the surface, and then settled, and the desert was quiet again, and India had entered, through that upheaval and that quiet, a category of nation it had not previously occupied.

The government of Indira Gandhi announced it as a peaceful nuclear explosive. Operation Smiling Buddha. The name had been chosen carefully, with the specific awareness of a government that understood the distinction between what it was doing and what it was saying it was doing, and the degree to which the international audience would and would not accept that distinction. A peaceful nuclear explosive — the phrase occupied an interesting semantic position, asserting civilian purpose while demonstrating military capability, claiming the one while producing the other, in the way that language sometimes tries to hold two contradictory things simultaneously and finds, for a brief moment, that the effort succeeds.

The international audience was not, by and large, persuaded.

· · ·

Arjun heard the news at Trombay at half past nine, when Krishnaswami appeared at the door of the calculation room with an expression that was difficult to read because it contained several things simultaneously — a controlled professional composure, and underneath it something that might have been pride, and underneath that something else entirely, something that the professional composure was working hard to keep where it belonged, which was interior and unspoken.

"It went well," Krishnaswami said. "The test. It has been confirmed."

Arjun set his pencil down. He had known the test was coming — not the date, the date was not known outside the very small circle that had been directly involved in the preparation, but the fact of it had been understood within the Atomic Energy Establishment for months, in the way that certain large institutional facts are understood without being officially communicated, through the accumulation of small signals that each individually mean nothing and together mean everything. He had known, and he had spent the months of knowing thinking about what it would mean, and now it had happened and the meaning he had been thinking about was no longer hypothetical.

"Thank you," he said to Krishnaswami.

Krishnaswami looked at him for a moment with the attention of a man trying to read a response that was more complicated than the words. Then he nodded and went to tell the next person. Arjun looked at the calculation sheet in front of him. He was working on a moderator analysis for the second heavy-water reactor, the one under construction at Rawatbhata in Rajasthan — not, as it happened, very far from the desert where the test had just occurred, a proximity that was geographical rather than programmatic but that his mind kept returning to with an insistence he found mildly irritating.

He put the calculation sheet to one side. He was not going to be able to work on it this morning, which was honest. He went instead to the window and looked out at the construction site, the cranes and the scaffolding and the concrete taking shape in the forms that would become the reactor buildings, and tried to think clearly about what had just happened and what it would mean.

What it would mean, in practical terms, was this. India had demonstrated a nuclear explosive device. The device had used plutonium — specifically, plutonium produced in the CIRUS research reactor at Trombay, which had been supplied by Canada in 1956 under an agreement that specified the reactor and its products were for peaceful purposes only. The use of that plutonium for a test device was, from Canada's point of view and from the point of view of the international nuclear community, a breach of the terms under which the cooperation had been extended. The response would be swift and it would be structural — not merely diplomatic displeasure but a reorganisation of the rules governing nuclear technology transfer, designed to ensure that what India had done could not be done again by any other country receiving civilian nuclear assistance. Within a year, the Nuclear Suppliers Group would be formed: a cartel of the countries with the technology to supply reactors and fuel and enrichment equipment, agreeing collectively that they would supply these things only to countries that accepted full international safeguards on all their nuclear activities. India, which had not signed the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty and had just demonstrated a weapon — peaceful explosive or not, the distinction was semantic from the supplier's point of view — would find the doors of that cartel closed to it.

The uranium imports that had been keeping the heavy-water reactors fuelled — the supply agreements with Canada, the arrangements with other countries that were possible because India had been a recipient of nuclear cooperation in good standing — would become complicated, and then difficult, and then, for a period, effectively impossible. The reactors would continue to run on the fuel already in them, and then on the domestically mined uranium that India had but not in sufficient quantities, and then on a combination of domestic ore and whatever could be negotiated through the increasingly narrow channels that remained open. The programme would not stop. But it would slow. And every year it slowed was a year added to the timeline that Bhabha had already stretched across a century.

This was what Arjun was thinking about at the window, watching the cranes move on the construction site. He was thinking about it with the specific quality of attention that a man brings to a problem he had foreseen and had been unable to prevent and is now watching arrive with the punctuality of things that were always going to happen.

He was not angry. He had considered, in the months of anticipation, whether he would be angry, and had found that anger was not the right response — not because the decision was right, he did not think it was right in the context of what it would cost the programme, but because anger required a specific person to be angry at, a specific decision-maker who had weighed the options and chosen the wrong one, and the situation was not that simple. The decision to test had been made in rooms he had never been in, by people operating under pressures he could only partially see, for reasons that included the programme he had given his career to and also included things that had nothing to do with it — regional security, domestic politics, the specific calculation of a government that had decided that India's position in the world required this demonstration at this time. He could think those calculations were wrong. He could not think the people who made them were simply careless.

What he felt, standing at the window on the morning of May the eighteenth, was something closer to grief than anger. The grief of a man who has spent nineteen years building the foundation of a thing that was designed to end a dependence, and who is watching a decision made in another room create a dependence more acute than the one he has been working to solve.

He thought: we spent twenty years trying to make India independent of other people's uranium. And now, because of this morning, we are going to be more dependent on other people's uranium than we have ever been, for longer than we had planned, with less international goodwill to smooth the arrangements. The irony was not subtle. It was the size of a desert.

He went back to his desk. He picked up his pencil. He looked at the moderator analysis and then set the pencil down again, because the honest answer was that he still could not work, and pretending otherwise would produce bad calculations, which was the one thing he was not willing to do regardless of what the morning had brought.

He opened the bottom drawer of his desk instead, where he kept the things he did not work on but sometimes needed — his father's letter about the Adyar bridge, a few photographs, a small transistor radio that he used on the evenings when he worked late and the institute was quiet. He took out the radio and put in the earpiece and turned it on quietly, not because he wanted news — he had the news, the news was the test, that was enough news for one morning — but because he needed something that was not the silence of the calculation room and not the institutional noise of the corridor, something that occupied the space between thinking and not thinking where the mind sometimes sorted itself out without being asked.

What came through, by the particular chance of the frequency and the time of day, was Carnatic music. A vocal performance — a woman's voice, high and precise and carrying in it something that the raga always carries when it is sung properly, which is the quality of an emotion given such exact form that the exactness itself becomes moving. He recognised the raga. Bhairavi. He had been listening to Carnatic music for years, since the evenings at the institute when the radio had first introduced him to it, but it had been in the last decade that it had become something he sought rather than received — going to concerts in Bombay when they were available, buying the recordings that the music shops on Lamington Road occasionally stocked, learning to hear what he was hearing with the informed attention that any form of music rewards when you bring it.

He had read about Bhairavi, in the way he read about things that interested him — with the thoroughness of an engineer who wants to understand the structure of what he is encountering. Bhairavi was the raga of endings and of the bittersweet — the raga traditionally performed last at a concert, the one that signalled that what had been given was now complete, that the music was returning the listener to the ordinary world. It was a raga of departure. Of the specific emotion of something that has been real and full and is now moving toward its close. It was not a sad raga, exactly. It was a raga that understood sadness as one of the things that life contained rather than an exception to it.

He listened for a long time.

Vikram arrived in Bombay four days later, in the last week of May, on the overnight bus from Pune where he was in his final year of economics at Fergusson College. He was twenty years old — thin, quick-minded, with his mother's eyes and his grandfather's habit of thinking before speaking, which in a twenty-year-old still looked like hesitation but would, in time, look like deliberateness. He had been planning the visit for weeks, a long weekend before his final examinations, but the timing had acquired a different significance than the one it had been planned with, and both of them understood this without saying so when Arjun met him at the bus stand on Saturday morning.

They ate breakfast at the Irani café near the bus stand — the one Arjun had been going to since 1955, that still had the same green-painted walls and the same slightly sticky tables and the same tea in glasses that were too hot to hold comfortably but which nobody ever put down, because the tea inside them was precisely right. Vikram ate with the appetite of a young man who had been on a bus overnight and ordered a second serving of the egg bhurji without being asked whether he wanted it, which was the right instinct. Arjun drank his tea and watched his son eat and found, in the watching, a quality of attention that he was not always able to bring to the people he loved — the attention of someone who is present rather than calculating, who has, for the moment, set aside the thing that has been occupying him and simply occupied the chair across from his son in an Irani café in Bombay on a Saturday morning.

Vikram was studying the political economy of the test. Of course he was — he was an economics student, the test was the largest economic and political event of the month, possibly the year, and Fergusson College was full of young men with opinions about it. He shared these opinions with the focused enthusiasm of a twenty-year-old who has recently discovered that the world's events connect to frameworks he has just learned, that the abstractions of the classroom are actually descriptions of the real. He talked about the NPT, about India's position as a non-signatory, about the geopolitical calculus of demonstrating capability versus the economic cost of international isolation. He was not wrong. He had read carefully and thought carefully and the analysis was sound as far as it went.

Arjun listened. He asked questions that pushed Vikram's analysis further rather than redirecting it, which was the mode he had always taken with his son's thinking — not correcting, but extending, finding the place where the argument needed to go further and asking the question that required it to go there. It was a mode he had learned from Bhabha, he recognised, which was perhaps inevitable.

"What about the fuel supply?" Arjun asked, after a while. "The civilian programme."

Vikram looked up from the remains of the egg bhurji. "The reactors?"

"The reactors need uranium. The uranium was coming, largely, from agreements with Canada and other countries. Those agreements were conditional on India maintaining what the international community considers acceptable nuclear behaviour. We have now done something that they do not consider acceptable behaviour."

Vikram thought about this. "So the supply gets cut."

"Complicated, at minimum. Reduced, very likely. Cut entirely, in some cases, yes."

"And that affects the power programme."

"That affects the power programme." Arjun paused. "We are building reactors at Rawatbhata. At Kalpakkam. The fuel for those reactors was to come from arrangements that are now in question. We have domestic uranium, but not in quantities sufficient for the programme as planned. The programme will slow." He said this without emphasis, as a statement of engineering fact, and Vikram heard it in that register — as information rather than complaint, as the kind of analysis his father offered when he was being honest rather than protective.

"How long does it slow for?"

Arjun looked at the glass of tea in his hands. "Long enough to matter," he said. "Years. Perhaps a decade or more before the international situation resolves sufficiently to allow normal cooperation again. In the meantime, we manage on what we have."

Vikram was quiet for a moment. He was looking at his father with the specific attention that young people bring to the moments when they understand, perhaps for the first time, that a parent's professional life contains difficulties of a kind and scale that domestic life does not reveal. "And you think the test was wrong," he said. It was not quite a question.

Arjun considered this with the care it deserved. "I think the test was the decision of people who were weighing things I was not in the room to weigh," he said. "I think the cost of it, for the programme I work on, is very high. Whether the things it was weighed against justified that cost — that is not a calculation I have access to." He paused. "What I know is the engineering. The engineering says the programme will be harder now. For a long time."

Vikram nodded slowly. Then, with the directness that was one of the things Arjun found most valuable about him: "Is it still worth doing? The programme. If it's going to be this complicated."

The Irani café was doing its Saturday morning business around them — the tables filling, the waiter moving between them with his particular economy of motion, the green walls holding their faded paint, the city outside the window conducting its ordinary Saturday. Arjun thought about Bhabha at the Geneva hotel desk, drawing the three boxes. He thought about the breeding ratio calculations, the folder with two sets of handwriting. He thought about the Kerala coastline, which he had never actually seen but which had been a part of his mental geography for nearly twenty years, the dark sand and the thorium that the dark sand contained.

"Yes," he said. "It is still worth doing."

He said it without hesitation and without drama, and Vikram, who had grown up understanding that his father did not say things he did not mean, received it as the answer it was — not reassurance, not performance, but the simple output of a man who had checked his own conviction, found it intact, and reported accordingly.

· · ·

They spent the rest of the day together in the ordinary way of a father and son who do not see each other often enough and have learned to be efficient with the time they have — a walk along the seafront at Marine Drive in the afternoon, the Arabian Sea doing its reliable thing in the heat, the city's skyline to the east, the water stretching west into the haze. They talked about Vikram's examinations, about what came after them, about the question of what a young man with an economics degree from Fergusson College did next in 1974 in a country that was simultaneously expanding rapidly and struggling with the specific constraints of its own political decisions.

There was a professor at Fergusson, Vikram mentioned, who had a connection to the University of Toronto — a visiting position there, an old academic relationship. The professor had mentioned to Vikram that there were postgraduate opportunities in Canada, in resource economics specifically, that might be worth exploring. Canada was building its resource sector, the oil sands in Alberta were attracting international attention, the uranium mining in Saskatchewan was expanding. For someone with an economics background who was interested in the intersection of natural resources and political economy, the opportunities were real and the timing was good.

Arjun listened to this without particular reaction, which was itself a form of reaction — the controlled neutrality of a man who is processing something that requires processing. Canada. The country whose nuclear cooperation India had just spent. The country whose uranium supply agreements were now in question. His son, talking about going to Canada to study resource economics at the moment when the resource that connected their two countries had become the source of a rupture.

He thought about saying this. He looked at his son — twenty years old, thin and quick, standing at the Marine Drive railing with the sea wind in his shirt — and considered the full arc of what he might say. He could explain the irony. He could describe the specific quality of the coincidence, the way the timing of the world's events and the timing of a young man's career decisions sometimes produced these collisions that had no authorship, that nobody had arranged but that nevertheless carried the feeling of meaning. He could say: the country you are thinking of going to is the country whose uranium we just spent, and that is a strange thing.

He said none of this. Vikram was twenty and his examinations were in three weeks and the Canada question was speculative, a possibility rather than a plan, and the correct thing to do with a speculative possibility in a young person's life was to leave it space to develop or not develop on its own terms, without loading it with the ironies of his father's professional situation.

"It sounds worth exploring," Arjun said. "If the professor can make the introduction, take it. Canada has good universities. The resource economics field is serious." He paused. "Talk to your mother before you decide anything."

Vikram smiled — the specific smile of a son who knows that talk to your mother is the formulation his father uses when he has given his own view and is now passing the baton to the domestic authority who will have the more detailed conversation. "I will," he said.

They stood at the railing for a while longer, looking at the sea. The afternoon was moving toward evening, the light beginning to take on the quality of the late Bombay afternoon, golden and slightly hazy, the kind of light that makes ordinary things look briefly significant, as though the day is trying to leave an impression before it goes. Arjun thought about the morning's news, and about the programme, and about the years ahead that would be harder than the years behind, and about the young man standing next to him who would go where he went and become what he became, and who had, in asking whether the programme was still worth doing, asked the right question in the right way at the right time, without knowing that he had done so.

The programme was worth doing. It would always be worth doing. The mathematics had not changed. The thorium had not moved. What had changed was the terrain between here and it, and the terrain being harder was not, in itself, a reason to stop moving.

· · ·

He wrote to his father that evening, after Vikram had gone to bed in the spare room. It was late, past eleven, the city outside quieter now, the harbour visible as a pattern of lights through the window. He wrote three pages in the deliberate way he wrote to his father — carefully, with the full intention of being understood rather than merely read, which was the mode the relationship between them had always required.

He described the test. His father would have heard the news but not the professional consequences, and Arjun laid these out with the engineering precision that was his natural mode — the fuel supply question, the international isolation, the timeline implications, the specific ways in which the years ahead would require the programme to find new solutions to problems that had seemed, a week ago, largely resolved. He described the conversation with Vikram, without the detail about Canada — that was Vikram's conversation to have with his grandfather in his own time — but with enough to convey that the boy was well and thinking seriously about his future and was, in Arjun's assessment, the kind of person who would figure out what he needed to figure out.

And then, in the last paragraph, he wrote something he had not planned to write when he began the letter, something that came from the evening rather than from the intention to communicate it:

From Arjun's letter to his father — May 18, 1974

What troubles me most is not the difficulty ahead — I have spent nineteen years learning that difficulty is the natural condition of this work, and I have made a kind of peace with that. What troubles me is the specific shape of this difficulty. We built this programme to free India from dependence. We built it because the alternative — relying on other countries for the fuel that runs our cities — was a dependence that independence had not ended. And now, because of this morning, we are more dependent on other countries' goodwill than we have been since we began. The programme that was supposed to be the solution has been, by a decision made in another room entirely, temporarily converted into part of the problem it was designed to solve.

I do not know how to feel about this except honestly. The work is still right. The physics is still right. The thorium is still on the coast. Nothing that happened today changes any of that. But the road to it has been made longer, and the making-longer was done by people who were not, I believe, thinking about the road at all.

I am well. The work continues. Vikram sends his love, though he does not know I am writing this.

He folded the letter and sealed it and put it on his desk to post in the morning. Then he sat for a while in the quiet room, the city doing its late-night things outside, the harbour lights on the water, the cranes of the construction site on the hill in the darkness.

He thought about Bhabha, which he still did on the difficult days — not as a source of guidance, exactly, because the dead do not guide, but as a reference point, a way of measuring the present against a standard that had been set clearly enough to be useful. He thought about what Bhabha had said in 1957, in the room with the blackboard and the diagram. The relay requires every section to be run. A runner who completes their section and passes the baton has done everything the race requires of them.

The section he was running had just become longer and harder. It had not become a different race. The baton was still in his hands. The direction was the same.

He went to bed. In the morning there were calculations to do, and a programme to run, and a country that had, in the course of one Saturday, both asserted its capability and complicated its future, and that would need, in the years ahead, the patient foundational work of the people who had been building quietly while the louder decisions were being made elsewhere.

In the months and years that followed, the consequences arrived with the methodical thoroughness of institutional responses to institutional provocations. Canada suspended nuclear cooperation with India in May 1974 and formalised the suspension through 1976. The heavy-water reactor at Rawatbhata, which had been running on Canadian-supplied uranium, faced fuel constraints that required rationing the reactor's operation. The second unit at the same site, under construction, proceeded at a pace that the fuel supply situation made simultaneously urgent — India needed the generating capacity — and difficult. The Kalpakkam reactor, being built on the southeastern coast, pressed ahead on domestic uranium that was stretched thin across the programme's needs.

In 1975, the Nuclear Suppliers Group was formalised — seven countries initially, expanding over the following years, agreeing collectively to the export conditions that would apply to nuclear technology and materials. India was not in the room when these conditions were written. India was the reason the room had been called.

The specific quality of the irony — noted here, never stated in the text The Nuclear Suppliers Group was formed, in direct response to India's test, to prevent the spread of nuclear weapons capability. Its mechanism was to restrict the supply of civilian nuclear technology and fuel to countries that did not accept full international safeguards. India, whose civilian nuclear programme had been designed from its foundations to eventually free it from dependence on uranium imports, now found that dependence made more acute and more politically fraught than it had ever been. The programme designed to solve the dependence problem had triggered the international response that deepened it. This was not anyone's intention. It was the outcome of several sets of rational decisions, each made within its own logic, combining to produce a result that none of the decision-makers had planned.

Arjun worked through it. He worked through it the way he worked through every constraint the programme had faced — by finding what was still possible within the new parameters and doing that, rather than spending energy on the parameters themselves, which were not within his control. The domestic uranium had to be managed more carefully. The reactor fuel cycles had to be optimised. The research programme on the thorium conversion chain — the work that was the real long-term answer, the work that would eventually make the uranium question irrelevant — had to be maintained even as resources were squeezed, because cutting the research programme was the one economy that would cost more in the long run than any short-term saving justified.

He argued for this repeatedly, in the budget meetings and the programme reviews and the conversations with the administrators who had to balance the immediate operational needs against the long-term research that had no immediate payoff. He was not always successful. He was successful enough. The research continued, thinner than it should have been, slower than the programme needed, but continuous — and continuity, as he had learned over twenty years of building a relay, was the thing that mattered most. A programme that slowed was recoverable. A programme that stopped was something else entirely.

He wrote to Vikram about none of this. Vikram had gone to Toronto in the autumn of 1975 — the professor's connection had materialised into a postgraduate offer, resource economics, the University of Toronto — and the letters between them had the character of correspondence between people who love each other and have different immediate realities and are doing their best to maintain a connection across the gap. Vikram wrote about Canada, about the university, about the extraordinary cold of a Toronto winter that no amount of prior description had adequately prepared him for. Arjun wrote about Bombay and the work and the ordinary texture of life at the institute, which was, he was aware, not the full picture but was the picture he could share without making his son feel responsible for a situation that was not his responsibility.

What Vikram was studying, in Toronto, was resource economics with a focus on mineral extraction and supply chain analysis. His particular interest, which had developed naturally from the combination of his supervisor's expertise and the specific character of the Canadian resource sector, was uranium. The economics of uranium mining. The supply chains. The geopolitics of fuel supply in the international nuclear industry. He was, in other words, becoming an expert in the precise resource that his father had spent his career trying to make India independent of.

Neither of them named this yet. There would come a time when it would be named, and the naming would be done quietly, between them, in the way that families acknowledge the ironies that life arranges without anyone's permission. But that time was not yet. Vikram was twenty-one and it was his first winter in Toronto and the snow was extraordinary and the work was good, and Arjun was forty-three and the programme was running on rationed fuel and the thorium was still on the coast and the relay was still moving, and both of these things were true simultaneously, and the truth of one did not diminish the truth of the other.

The desert at Pokhran was quiet again. The sand had settled back. The device was gone, its work done, its consequences still unfolding in the rooms of international institutions and the fuel supply ledgers of a nuclear programme that had been trying, for twenty years, to make itself independent of exactly the kind of morning that had just passed.

The work continued. It always continued. That was the only answer the programme had, and it was, in the long run, the right one.
