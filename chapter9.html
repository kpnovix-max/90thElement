Chapter Nine
Kalpakkam,
2014–2022
2014 – 2022
"He was eighty-two years old and the reactor he had
spent his life on was still not finished."

Retirement had suited Arjun better than he had expected, which was partly a comment on retirement and partly a comment on the specific quality of Madras in the years after he returned to it. He had come back to the house his mother had left him — a house in Mylapore that his wife Meenakshi had made her own over thirty years of marriage, filling it with the smell of her cooking and the sound of the Carnatic recordings she played on weekend mornings and the particular order she imposed on rooms that had been bachelor-sparse before she arrived. She had died in 2009, two years before his retirement, and the house had reorganised itself around her absence in the small ways that houses do — quieter in the mornings, the kitchen managed now by habit rather than by the pleasure she had taken in it. The city had changed, as all Indian cities had changed in the decade after the economy opened — it was Chennai now, officially, had been Chennai since 1996, and the new name sat alongside the old one in the way that new names for old places always sit, coexisting with the previous version in the mouths of people who had grown up with the older one and used the newer one in formal contexts and could not always tell you which they were doing at any given moment. The city had grown outward, the way Indian cities grew in those years — not replacing what was there but surrounding it, the old neighbourhoods holding their character while new districts assembled themselves around them, the roads overwhelmed by the traffic that prosperity had generated, the air carrying the specific combination of exhaust and sea salt and jasmine that was the city's persistent signature regardless of what else it added to itself.

He lived in the house where he had grown up, which his mother had left to him when she died in 2006, the year he turned seventy-four, ninety-one years old and clear-minded to the end, asking him on the last coherent day whether he had eaten and whether the programme was progressing. He had told her yes to both, truthfully on the first count and approximately on the second, and she had said good and closed her eyes. He had been grateful for the house — not as a possession but as a continuity, a place in which several decades of his life had already happened and in which the walls carried the accumulated evidence of that happening in the way that old family houses do, the height measurements pencilled on the doorframe from when he was a child, the particular quality of the courtyard light in the afternoon that he had known since before he could name it. He had lived in government quarters and institute dormitories and the Trombay residential blocks for most of his career. This was home in a different sense — the original sense, the one that all the subsequent ones had been measured against.

He read. He attended Carnatic concerts, which Chennai hosted in extraordinary abundance, the December music season filling the city with performances that ran from morning to midnight across dozens of venues, the accumulated musical wealth of a tradition that had been growing and deepening for centuries and showed no sign of exhaustion. He listened with the informed attention of a man who had been serious about the music for forty years and had, in that time, developed the specific pleasure of the experienced listener — the pleasure of knowing what was being done and being surprised by it anyway, of hearing a performer find something in a raga that previous performances had not found, of being taken somewhere by music that had carried other people to other places and was now carrying him to his. He went to the Thyagaraja Festival at Thiruvaiyaru each year when his health permitted, the five-day celebration on the banks of the Kaveri River where the saint-composer had lived and died, thousands of musicians converging on a small town to sing the same compositions that Arjun had been listening to since the transistor radio in his desk at Trombay in the 1970s. He had been going since 2004. It was one of the best things in his retired life, which was a life with several good things in it.

He was not following the PFBR construction closely, or not as closely as he had followed the programme during his working years. He read the DAE annual reports when Rajan sent them — Rajan had been promoted twice since Arjun's retirement and was now leading the fast reactor systems engineering group, which was the group directly responsible for the PFBR's construction — and the annual reports told him what the programme wanted the public record to show, which was progress at a pace that was encouraging without being overconfident. He knew, from reading between the lines the way you read the reports of a programme you have spent fifty years inside, that the pace was slower than encouraging and that the specific technical difficulties of building a sodium-cooled fast reactor at commercial scale were asserting themselves with the thoroughness that such difficulties always brought to projects that had underestimated them in the planning phase.

He was not troubled by this. Large engineering programmes ran slower than their plans. That was not a failure of the programme. It was a property of large engineering programmes, as consistent and as predictable as any physical constant, and the appropriate response to it was the response the programme had always required — patience, and the continued quality of the work, and the faith that the distance was real and not permanent.

Rajan called him in February of 2014. The call was professional in its framing — a technical question, a request for a consultancy visit — but underneath the professional framing was the call of a man who has encountered something he has not encountered before and who knows someone who has encountered more things than he has.

"There is a weld problem," Rajan said. "In the primary circuit. The failure mode is unusual. I have read everything we have and I cannot find a prior case."

Arjun thought about this. "Send me what you have," he said. "The failure documentation, the material specifications, the operating conditions at the time of failure. Send me everything."

The documents arrived that evening, by email. He read them on the laptop that Vikram had bought him two Christmases ago and that he had learned to use with the careful deliberateness of someone who came to a technology in his seventies and was not embarrassed about taking the time it required. He read the failure report carefully, then the material specifications, then the records of the operating conditions. He made notes in the margin of the printed pages, which was still his preferred mode of annotation, the pen in his hand more reliable than the keyboard for the kind of thinking that needed to happen on the page.

Sodium coolant in a fast breeder reactor is an extraordinary engineering medium — it transfers heat with exceptional efficiency, it does not slow the fast neutrons that the breeding process requires, and it remains liquid across the temperature range that the reactor operates in. It is also, as the engineers who work with it discover at regular intervals, a material that does not forgive errors of design or operation. Sodium reacts violently with both air and water — not quickly but energetically, burning on contact with air and producing explosive hydrogen gas on contact with water. A sodium leak in a primary circuit is not merely a maintenance problem. It is a safety event that must be managed with the specific care of people who understand that the liquid flowing through the pipes at five hundred degrees Celsius will, if it reaches an uncontrolled environment, behave in ways that no amount of theoretical preparation fully prepares you for. The weld failure in the primary circuit of the PFBR was in a section of pipework that carried sodium from the reactor core to the intermediate heat exchanger — a critical path, operating under thermal stresses that cycled with every change in reactor power level. The failure mode was a form of stress corrosion cracking that occurred at the grain boundaries of the steel at the weld interface — a failure that required a very specific combination of thermal cycling frequency, salt chemistry, and weld microstructure to produce, and that was sufficiently rare that it was not in the standard design literature for sodium-cooled systems.

He found it, eventually, in the Japanese fast reactor programme documentation. The Monju reactor, which had been built in Fukui Prefecture and had been the subject of a serious sodium leak in 1995 that had set the Japanese programme back by years, had produced a technical investigation report that included, in an appendix, a characterisation of secondary failure modes in sodium circuit welds that had been observed during the investigation. The failure mode in the PFBR documents matched, closely enough to be significant, one of the failure types described in Appendix C of the Monju investigation.

He had read the Monju report in 1996, in the months after the leak, because it was available in the international nuclear safety literature and he read everything in the international nuclear safety literature that was relevant to fast reactor design, because that was what his work required and because the habit of reading everything was the habit that had built his understanding over fifty years. He had not expected to need the appendix to a 1996 Japanese accident investigation report in 2014. He had read it anyway.

He called Rajan the following morning. He described what he had found. He cited the specific section of the Monju report. He described the weld redesign that the Japanese investigation had recommended, which addressed the grain boundary vulnerability by changing the thermal treatment of the weld interface. He explained the connection between the thermal cycling frequency and the crack propagation rate, which was the diagnostic key — if the cycling frequency matched the resonant frequency of the grain boundary fatigue mechanism, the cracks grew faster than the standard models predicted, which was why the failure had appeared earlier and in a different location than the design envelope specified.

There was a silence on the other end of the call. Then Rajan said: "You read the Monju appendices."

"I read everything," Arjun said. "You should also read everything."

"I will start reading appendices," Rajan said, and there was something in his voice that was not quite humour and not quite relief but was in the vicinity of both.

The weld was redesigned over the following three months. The thermal treatment specification was updated. The section of primary circuit containing the failed weld was replaced with the new design. Construction resumed. The crisis had cost the programme time — several months, which would be added to the timeline that was already stretched — but it had not compromised the reactor's fundamental design. The relay had needed something from Arjun one more time, and the relay had received it, and the relay had continued.

Arjun went back to Madras on the evening train. He watched the Tamil Nadu coast out the window — the flat land, the palm trees, the occasional glint of the sea to the east. He ate a thali from the station canteen, which was adequate, and read for a while, and fell asleep somewhere south of Villupuram, and woke as the train was pulling into Chennai Central in the early morning, the city assembling itself in the grey pre-dawn light outside the window.

He took an autorickshaw home. The driver was young and drove fast and played film music on a phone mounted to the dashboard, the bass frequencies filling the autorickshaw with a cheerful urgency that was entirely at odds with five-thirty in the morning but that Arjun found, to his own mild surprise, pleasant rather than irritating. The city was coming awake. The milk vendors on bicycles. The temple opening its gates for the morning prayers, the sound of the bells carrying across the street. The jasmine sellers arranging their baskets at the corner. He had left this city at twenty-three with a canvas bag and an annotated textbook. He had returned to it at seventy-one with everything the intervening years had put in him. The city had been here the whole time, doing what it did, indifferent to his absence and unremarkable about his return.

He liked it for that. He had always liked it for that.

Rajan called him back in 2019. Not for a crisis this time — for a ceremony, or something close to one, though the word ceremony was not used because Rajan was a precise man who did not use words loosely. What he said was: the thorium blanket assembly is being installed next month. I thought you should see it before it goes in. If you are willing to come.

Arjun said he was willing to come.

The drive from Chennai to Kalpakkam was forty minutes on a good day and an hour and a half on a bad one, and on the morning of the visit it was neither — a Tuesday, mid-morning, the traffic on the East Coast Road moving in the way that traffic on that road moved, which was with the specific mixture of discipline and improvisation that characterised Indian road travel and that Arjun had long since stopped finding remarkable. The Bay of Bengal was visible on the right for most of the drive, the water a deep blue-green in the October morning light, the occasional fishing boat far out. Rajan had sent a car. The driver was young and quiet and drove with his attention on the road, which was everything you needed a driver to be.

The Kalpakkam Nuclear Complex had grown considerably since Arjun's retirement. The MAPS units were running. The Fast Breeder Test Reactor, which had achieved criticality in 1985 and had been running since, was now a mature facility, its earlier experimental uncertainties resolved into the routine of an operational reactor. And the PFBR site — the main event, the reason the complex had been building toward this moment for four decades — was a construction landscape of considerable scale: the reactor building rising from its foundations, the auxiliary systems buildings surrounding it, the sodium storage facilities, the electrical systems, the cooling systems, all of it connected by the piping and cabling that was the circulatory system of a large nuclear facility, still being installed, still incomplete, but recognisably and indisputably taking the shape of the thing it was going to be.

Rajan met him at the gate. He was fifty now — the young engineer from IIT Madras who had asked whether Stage 3 would come in their lifetimes was fifty years old, with grey at his temples and the specific settled confidence of a man who has been doing important work for a long time and has stopped needing to demonstrate its importance. They shook hands with the warmth of people who have a shared history that does not require rehearsing. Rajan said: you look well. Arjun said: you look like you haven't slept in a week. Rajan smiled and said: accurate.

They walked to the assembly hall.

The thorium blanket assembly was housed in a high-ceilinged building on the eastern side of the PFBR site, a clean room environment maintained at the temperature and humidity specifications that the assembly process required. They were given the protective gear — the coveralls, the gloves, the shoe covers — and Arjun put it on with the practiced ease of someone who has worn protective gear in nuclear facilities for fifty years and has no particular feeling about it one way or the other. Then they went in.

The blanket assemblies were arranged on racks along the far wall — long cylindrical rods of thorium oxide, contained in steel cladding, assembled into the hexagonal bundles that would be installed in the annular region surrounding the reactor core. There were hundreds of them. The room smelled of metal and of the specific clean neutrality of a controlled environment. The lighting was bright and even, the kind of lighting that exists to reveal rather than to illuminate, that makes objects visible in their exact condition rather than lending them the mood that ordinary light tends to lend. Under this light the thorium rods were precisely what they were — dull grey cylinders, unimpressive in appearance, carrying nothing that could be seen or smelled or heard to indicate what they contained or what they were going to be made to do.

Rajan walked him along the rack. He explained the assembly specifications — the enrichment level of the thorium oxide, the cladding material and its thermal properties, the geometry of the hexagonal bundles and the way they would fit into the blanket region around the core. He spoke with the fluency of someone explaining work he knows completely, and Arjun listened with the attention of someone who knows the same work from a different angle, from the calculations rather than the physical objects, and was now seeing the physical objects for the first time.

At the end of the rack Rajan paused. He looked at Arjun. He said: "Would you like to hold one?"

It was an unusual thing to ask. The assemblies were not museum pieces. They were components in an industrial process, regulated and documented and handled according to procedures that existed for good reasons. But there was no procedure that prohibited what Rajan was suggesting — the assemblies were not yet in the radiation environment, they were stored material, and holding one briefly in gloved hands caused nothing that the handling procedures did not already account for.

Arjun said yes.

Rajan lifted one of the rods from its rack position — a single rod, not a full assembly, perhaps sixty centimetres long, the diameter of a large man's thumb — and held it for a moment, and then passed it to Arjun.

Kalpakkam Assembly Hall — October 2019 — the relay in physical form He took it in both hands, through the gloves. It was heavier than it looked — thorium oxide was a dense material, denser than the steel cladding that enclosed it, and the rod had the specific solid weight of something that contained more than its size suggested. He held it at about chest height and looked at it. A grey cylinder. Dull surface. The steel cladding smooth and unmarked. Nothing about its appearance announced what it was or what it would become. It was the same thorium that had been in the Kerala sand and the Tamil Nadu monazite deposits for four and a half billion years, refined now, formed into this precise shape, waiting in this clean room for the installation that would place it around the reactor core where the neutrons would begin to reach it. He thought about the Geneva hotel desk in 1954, the three boxes and the arrows, the diagram that Bhabha had drawn after playing Schubert, the programme that had been designed in that room and that had produced, across seventy years, this object in his hands. He thought about his father's letter: the question was whether the bridge was there for them to repair. The bridge was here. He was holding one of its components. He was eighty-seven years old and he was holding the thorium that Bhabha had planned to use in 1954, and that his own breeding ratio calculations in 1966 had been designed to make accessible, and that Rajan's sodium dynamics work and a hundred other people's work over thirty years had been converging toward, and it was a grey cylinder in his gloved hands in a clean room forty kilometres south of Madras and it weighed, he estimated, perhaps four hundred grams, and the weight of it was exactly the weight of four hundred grams of thorium oxide in a steel cladding tube, and also the weight of everything else that the programme had been, and both of these things were true simultaneously and neither of them diminished the other.

He handed it back to Rajan. He said: "Good cladding work." Which was the correct technical observation and also the only thing he was going to say, because anything else would have been too much.

Rajan received this in the way it was intended. He replaced the rod in its rack position with care. They stood for a moment in the bright even light of the assembly hall, not saying anything, because the moment did not require speech and both of them knew it.

Then Rajan said: "The installation begins in six weeks. The reactor building will be sealed after that. This was the last time."

"I know," Arjun said. "That is why I came."

They walked back out through the clean room exit, through the decontamination procedure, back into the ordinary Tamil Nadu morning — the sea smell, the construction noise from the PFBR site, a kite riding the thermal above the reactor building, turning in slow circles without apparent purpose. Arjun stood in the morning for a moment and felt the specific quality of a moment that has been complete in itself, that does not need to be anything more than what it was.

They had lunch together in the facility canteen — rice and sambar and the excellent rasam that the Kalpakkam canteen had always made, which was one of the things Arjun genuinely missed from his working years, the rasam at lunchtime on a working day, nothing theatrical about it, just the right thing made correctly. They talked about the programme — the fuel loading schedule, the construction completion timeline, the sodium filling that would follow, the steps that led from this assembly hall to first criticality, which Rajan estimated at somewhere between two and three years, depending on the resolution of the remaining construction issues.

"You will be there," Arjun said. It was not a question.

"I will be there," Rajan said. "And I will call you. I told you I would and I will."

"I know," Arjun said. "I will be in Chennai."

"Watching on a screen."

"Watching on a screen," Arjun confirmed. He said it without regret — or rather, with a regret that had been examined and accepted and converted into something that was not quite satisfaction but was adjacent to it. He had held the thorium rod. He would watch the criticality on a screen. Both of these were correct. Both of these were what the relay required of him at this stage, and the relay's requirements were not his to negotiate.

The COVID lockdowns began in March of 2020, which paused the PFBR construction and paused most other things as well, and Arjun spent the spring and summer of that year at home in Madras in the specific containment of an old man in a pandemic, which was not entirely different from his normal life in its outward structure — he had always been more interior than exterior, had always preferred the house and the books and the music to the obligations that took him out of them — but which had a different quality of feeling, an awareness of the world outside operating under conditions that required the people in it to make specific calculations about risk and distance and what could and could not be done that he found both serious and, at times, slightly surreal when considered against the eighty-eight years of his experience, which had contained other conditions and other calculations of different kinds.

Vikram called in April. A video call on WhatsApp, which had become their medium of regular communication — better than the letters of earlier decades, less formal, carrying something closer to actual presence, the face on the screen more information than words on a page. Vikram was sixty-six now, in Vancouver, where he had moved from Toronto fifteen years ago when his company — the uranium mining company's strategic planning division where he had worked for thirty years — had relocated its headquarters to the West Coast. He had a house there, a daughter who was at the University of British Columbia, a life that was Canadian in all its substantial details while remaining, in some interior register, connected to the Madras house and the father who lived in it.

They talked for an hour. The pandemic, and the specific ways in which Canada and India were experiencing it differently. Vikram's daughter, who was in her second year of environmental engineering. The construction pause at Kalpakkam, which Arjun mentioned briefly as a feature of the current situation. His own health, which was good enough for eighty-eight, which was the honest answer. His mother's cooking, which Vikram said he missed — meaning Meenakshi's cooking, the specific dishes she had made on his visits home as a young man, which were now available only in memory and in the occasional attempt by Vikram's own daughter to reconstruct them from recollection — which was the kind of thing that people say when they have been away for long enough that the things they miss have moved from specific objects to the category of things the specific objects represented.

Near the end of the call, Vikram mentioned something he had been meaning to mention for a while, he said, but which the timing had not been right for. His company — and here there was a very slight shift in register, from son to professional, the almost imperceptible change of gear that Arjun had learned to hear over the decades — had been conducting a long-term analysis of the global nuclear fuel market, specifically the question of what happened to uranium demand under different scenarios for thorium reactor deployment. The PFBR at Kalpakkam, and the Chinese TMSR programme at Wuwei, and the various smaller programmes elsewhere, were all feeding into a model that the company's strategic team had been building for three years.

WhatsApp video call — April 2020 — the irony named, quietly, between them

"The basic finding," Vikram said, with the careful neutrality of a man delivering a professional assessment, "is that if thorium technology scales — if what you are building at Kalpakkam works and if the Chinese experiment works and if the technology moves into commercial deployment over the next twenty or thirty years — then uranium demand in the second half of the century looks very different from uranium demand in the first half. Not collapse. The transition is too slow for collapse. But significant reduction, over time, in the countries that successfully deploy thorium reactors." He paused. "Which is strategically important for us. We need to be positioned for that scenario."

Arjun listened to this. He listened to the professional register, and to the content, and to the specific position from which his son was speaking — the position of a person whose livelihood was connected to the continued demand for the fuel that his father had spent his career trying to make India independent of. He listened to all of this without interrupting.

When Vikram finished, there was a silence of perhaps four seconds. Then Arjun said: "That is a thorough analysis."

Vikram looked at him through the screen. "Appa." A single word, the Tamil word for father that he had used as a child and that still appeared, occasionally, when the formal register dropped. "I know."

"I know you know," Arjun said.

Another silence. Then Vikram said: "Is the programme going to succeed? Truly. Not the official answer."

And Arjun said: "Yes. It is going to succeed. It will take longer than anyone wants and it will cost more than anyone plans and it will succeed. The physics is right. It has always been right."

Vikram nodded slowly. He did not say anything for a moment. Then: "I know that too." He said it quietly, and it was not an admission of anything and not an apology for anything — it was simply a true thing, stated plainly, between two people who had been circling it for a long time and had arrived, in a pandemic April in 2020, at the place where it could be said.

They ended the call with the ordinary closings — stay safe, call your daughter, eat properly, yes I know, I love you, I love you too, goodbye. The screen went dark. Arjun set his phone on the table beside the chair and sat in the house in Madras that his mother had left him, in the room where the afternoon light came in from the courtyard at an angle he had known since childhood, and thought about his son.

He thought about the train platform in Madras in 1955, his father watching the train leave. He thought about the letter from Toronto in 1978 mentioning the conference on uranium supply chains. He thought about the Marine Drive afternoon in 1974, the sea wind, his choice not to name what he could see. He thought about forty-five years of telephone calls and letters and now video calls, the distance between them maintained and crossed and maintained again, the love never in question and the daily knowledge of each other always slightly less than either of them would have chosen.

He thought about the thorium rod in his hands in the assembly hall, and about the programme that had produced it, and about the fact that his son had spent his career on the other side of the equation — the uranium side, the supply side, the side that the thorium programme was designed to eventually make unnecessary — and that this had happened without plan, without intention, through the ordinary operation of a young man's choices accumulating into a life, and that the irony of it was not diminished by being nobody's fault. It was simply what had happened. The programme and the man had gone in opposite directions and had arrived, in a video call during a pandemic lockdown, at the place where both directions were visible simultaneously.

Vikram knew. He had always known, at some level. The knowing and the working had coexisted, as knowing and working often coexisted in a life that was larger than any single dimension of it. Arjun had spent fifty years on one side of a fuel equation. His son had spent forty-five on the other. Both of them had done their work well. The equation was not yet resolved. The thorium programme was moving toward its resolution, and the resolution, when it came, would change the equation, and the changing of the equation would be part of what the programme had always been designed to produce. That was not a tragedy. It was a consequence. And the consequence was, in the long view, the right one.

He sat in the afternoon light for a long time. Then he put on the music — Darbari Kanada on the old CD player that sat on the shelf where the transistor radio had once sat, Ramanathan's voice filling the room with the deep-night raga, the music of long experience and the specific kind of beauty that is not arrived at quickly, that requires everything that has preceded it to be fully heard. He listened until the CD ended. Then he sat in the silence that followed, which was, as the silence after great music always is, both an ending and a continuation.

The construction resumed in late 2020 as the pandemic's most acute phase passed and the site returned to its working condition. The fuel loading — the actual insertion of the MOX fuel assemblies into the reactor core, the process that would be followed by the sodium filling and the approach to criticality — was scheduled to begin in late 2025. The thorium blanket assemblies, installed in 2019 and 2020, were already in position around the core region, waiting for the neutrons that the operating reactor would produce, waiting to begin the conversion that the programme had been built to enable.

Arjun followed this from Madras, from the DAE updates that Rajan sent him and from the news reports that appeared periodically as the programme's progress attracted the attention that large national engineering projects attracted when they neared their completion. He read these reports with the dual attention of someone who knew the programme from the inside and was now reading it from the outside — the expert's eye and the ordinary reader's eye operating simultaneously, the expert noting what the reports got right and what they simplified, the ordinary reader receiving the news of something large approaching its moment.

He was ninety-two years old. His health was what it was at ninety-two — the body's accumulated negotiations with time producing a set of conditions that required management and that were being managed, with the help of the doctor who visited every two weeks and the housekeeper who came every day and the food that Vikram's daughter, who had taken a position in Bangalore after her graduation and visited Chennai once a month, brought from the market because she had decided, without being asked, that this was something she wanted to do and that her great-uncle — she called him uncle, though the relationship was technically great-uncle — deserved home-cooked food on a regular basis, which was a decision Arjun received with gratitude and a complete absence of argument.

She was twenty-four years old. She had her father's eyes and her grandmother's directness and a career in environmental engineering that was, in its own way, working on the same problem from a different angle — the problem of how a civilisation powered itself without destroying the conditions that made the civilisation possible. She did not know the full history of the PFBR programme, not in the detail that Arjun knew it, but she knew the outline, and she asked about it with genuine interest, and he told her about it with the pleasure of an old man who has found a young person worth talking to and has the time to do so.

He told her about Geneva in 1954. About the diagram. About Bhabha and the three boxes and the arrows. About Apsara going critical in 1956 and what the control room looked like that day and what Bhabha had said, which was good, just that. He told her about the Pokhran test and what it had cost the programme and why it had happened anyway. He told her about the invisible decades, the fuel shortages and the budget compressions and the engineers who had shown up every day for twenty-five years when nothing dramatic was happening and had built the foundation that made this moment possible. He told her about the thorium rod in his hands in the assembly hall.

She listened with the full attention of someone who understands that what they are hearing will not be available to hear again from this particular source. She asked good questions. She understood the physics well enough to follow the technical parts, and she understood the human parts because she was human and the human parts were not complicated — they were simply what it looked like when a person spent a life on something that mattered and had the patience to see it through to the relay, if not to the finish.

On one of these evenings, in the autumn of 2022, she asked him: "Are you glad you stayed? That you chose this instead of London, instead of the private sector, instead of — all of it?"

He considered the question with the honesty it deserved, which took a moment, because honest answers to questions about a whole life are not assembled quickly. Then he said: "I am glad I did the work that needed doing. Whether I am glad about the choosing — that question assumes the choosing was the point. The choosing was just the beginning. The work was the point."

She looked at him for a moment. Then she said: "That is a very physicist's answer."

"Yes," he said. "I suppose it is."

He smiled. She smiled back. Outside the courtyard the Chennai evening was doing what Chennai evenings did — the temperature dropping to something that deserved to be called pleasant, the sounds of the street carrying through the gate, the smell of the jasmine that someone in the neighbourhood grew and that sent its fragrance over the wall at this hour, reliable and generous and asking nothing.

In the clean room at Kalpakkam, forty kilometres down the coast, the thorium blanket assemblies were in position around the reactor core, installed, sealed, waiting. The fuel loading was fourteen months away. The criticality was perhaps two years after that. The relay was in the final metres of its current section, moving toward the moment when everything the programme had been built toward would begin its physical expression — not complete, not Stage 3, not the destination, but the essential step before the destination, the step that made the destination real rather than planned.

The element waited in the rods as it had always waited, in the sand and the mountains and the mines and now in the precision-machined cylinders in the sealed reactor vessel — patient, dense, ancient beyond any reckoning that the human timescale could hold, indifferent to the decades and the decades and the decades it had taken to arrive at this moment and entirely prepared, when the moment arrived, to do what it had always been capable of doing.
